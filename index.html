Choose a narrator

Pick a system voice, or paste a recorded narration URL (LibriVox, archive.org, your own MP3) to replace the synthetic reader entirely.
Look for voices marked "Premium," "Enhanced," or "Neural" — they sound noticeably less robotic. Leave on Auto to let Lumen pick a voice that matches each book (British for the gothic works, American for Douglass and Sinclair, female where the narrator is a young woman).
If set, Lumen plays this audio instead of synthesizing. Use a LibriVox or archive.org link.
Each book has its own tuned base rate; this multiplies it. You can also adjust on the fly with the − / + buttons in the reader.
A note on voices. The mainstream open-source TTS engines (Piper, Coqui, MeloTTS) don't currently ship voice models specifically modeled on Black American speech — a real gap. The strongest open-source path is voice cloning (Coqui XTTS-v2, OpenVoice, F5-TTS), which can clone any narrator from a short sample but needs a small backend with a GPU. For canonical texts, the best near-term answer is licensing real human recordings — many already exist in the public domain on LibriVox and archive.org. Paste one above and Lumen will use it.
